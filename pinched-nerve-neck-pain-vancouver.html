Neck & nerve pain

Pinched nerve in the neck: what it is, and how recovery usually unfolds

Neck pain that travels into the shoulder, arm or hand — often with tingling, numbness or weakness — is frequently a pinched nerve in the neck, known clinically as cervical radiculopathy. The reassuring part: the large majority of cases settle without surgery over a matter of weeks to months.

What's actually happening

A nerve root leaves the spinal cord and exits between two neck vertebrae through a small bony opening. When a disc bulges or that opening narrows, the nerve can become compressed and irritated — so you feel symptoms not just in the neck but anywhere along that nerve's path down the arm. Which finger tingles often points to which level is involved.

A key recovery signal: "centralization." As an irritated nerve calms, pain often retreats up the limb — fingers and forearm quiet down first, and symptoms pull back toward the neck and shoulder. Pain moving closer to the spine, even before it's fully gone, is generally a good sign you're on the right track.

A typical recovery timeline

Every nerve recovers on its own schedule, and these phases overlap — but this is the shape recovery tends to take, and where physiotherapy helps at each stage.

  • Settling the irritation  Weeks 0–2

    Symptoms are often at their loudest, sometimes sharp with certain movements or first thing in the morning. The job here is to calm the nerve, not push through it: protected positions, sleep set-up, gentle pain-free movement, and managing pain so you can rest.

  • Restoring movement  Weeks 2–6

    The flare usually starts to ease and symptoms may begin to centralize. Hands-on manual therapy, posture and gentle nerve-gliding work restore range of motion while keeping the nerve happy. Many people regain meaningful day-to-day function in this window.

  • Rebuilding strength  Weeks 6–12

    With pain more controlled, the focus shifts to rebuilding deep neck and shoulder-blade strength and graded return to work, lifting and activity — the part that makes recovery hold rather than relapse.

  • Remodelling & resolution  3–6 months

    Disc-related compression often continues to resolve over months as the body reabsorbs the bulge. Residual numbness or tingling can be the last thing to fade and may trail the pain — usually a sign the nerve is still recovering, not a setback.

Timeline is a general guide for typical disc-related cases, not a promise — chronic or severe compression can take longer, and your physiotherapist will calibrate to what your nerve is telling us.

When to seek urgent care

Most pinched nerves are not dangerous, but see a doctor promptly if you develop progressive weakness, symptoms in both arms or both legs, problems with balance or coordination, or any change in bladder or bowel control.

Will I need surgery?
Usually not. Most cervical radiculopathy improves with conservative care; surgery is reserved for cases that don't settle or have progressive neurological loss.
Should I rest or keep moving?
Gentle, pain-free movement generally beats strict rest. The aim is to stay active within a range that doesn't stir the nerve up.
Why is my hand still tingling when the pain is gone?
Sensory symptoms often recover more slowly than pain. Lingering tingling that's stable or shrinking is typically part of normal nerve healing.
